The Lonely Halls

“I’m not here.”

“I’m NOT here.”

“I’M NOT HERE!”

And he wasn’t. The battle continued, where he had been, but he was gone. Much more gone than usual, which wouldn’t be realized by others until the battle had passed.

It was black, pitch black. Somewhere he heard a slow dripping sound and the quiet buzz of fluorescent lights, but it was black that surrounded him as his mind slowly cleared. The quiet, even with the dripping sound, helped to calm his panicked mind.

“Slow, slow.” he thought, “Breathe in, hold breathe out.”

No impacts, no new pain. His mind slowly calmed.

With calmness, came realization. The dark was because his eyes were closed. The concept of choice pushed the restarting of his mind. To open or not to open. A quiet giggle slid out of him. What would Shakespeare think of the rewording. Quiet laughter at himself followed.

Slowly he opened his eyes and quickly re-closed them. The bright white was blinding. Slowly he sat up, he didn’t sense danger, but kept his guard up, listening carefully to what was going on around him. The quiet remained, not pressing, but omnipresent. He slowly reopened his eyes, allowing them to adjust.

Looking around, it wasn’t as bright as he thought. There were fluorescent lights, the walls reflecting a brilliant sterile white. The room was small, a white door with a silver knob was in front of him.

Moving carefully, feeling every bruise and ache, he sat up. Too much practice had him checking himself for injuries. One slash through his jacket sleeve and several bruises were quickly found. A deep breath made his ribs ache.

“Ouch.” he quietly gasped, “No deep breaths for a while.”

His breath caught, his condition checked, now time to decide what to do next. The room itself was empty. The room was empty, nothing but him, some small smears of blood on the floor, and a door. Glancing down, the face of the cheap timex watch was shattered.

Rather than releasing the tension built up, panic started to build. This was not where he had been. He didn’t feel safe; questions bombarded his mind - “How did he get here?”, “Where was he?”, and “Who’s watching?” As his mind started to spiral and his vision darkened, he dropped to sit on the floor, his head between his hands. The light blocked, he started deep breathing. In for five, hold, out for five, repeat.

“I can feel the floor, my jeans, the pain in my ribs, the watch on my wrist, the tightness of my shoes on my feet.” he continued, “I can smell the blood from my scratches, I can smell myself, I can smell the leather of my boots. Taste, my mouth tastes like something died in it. I can hear, I can hear …. not much.”

Time passed. He’d been focused on his breathing, not time itself. He knew time had passed, but not how much. The room hadn’t changed, nothing had come in or gone out. There was no indication of what the room was for, no contents to give context. He straightened up, his back aching from being hunched. He cautiously stretched, ribs were still tight and there were more smears on the floor. The scrapes and scratches had quit bleeding. Standing he straightened himself up as best he could, checking his pockets as he shifted clothing to more comfortable positions. Finding his pager in a pocket, he smiled.

The smile quickly disappeared though. The screen of the pager was blank and adjusting the battery didn’t bring it back to life. Similarly, the old walkman in another pocket showed no signs of life.

“What are the odds?” he quietly wondered. He remembered using the Walkman earlier in the evening. He’d scanned the dials looking for the minds around him before they went over the fence. It has worked then and the power had started his inspection in the off position.

Pulling himself together, he went to turn off the light in the room. He was going to have to open the door, at least a little bit. Being backlit was a bad idea - he’d seen that in action earlier in the evening. He was really hoping that when he opened the door a large hairy arm wouldn’t force it further and then yank him out. It happened to a guard and all the body armor hadn’t helped at all. He’d seen lobsters take longer to crack and peel.

After looking twice, he confirmed that no light switch had been installed in the room. Unhappily he contemplated his options. After careful thought, he slid behind the door and tried the knob. He was happy to not hear any noise and to feel it freely turn in his hand. Slowly he cracked the door open, carefully looking around it to see beyond it.

Nothing with teeth, nothing without teeth. He could see a short distance down a hallway. The identical fluorescent fixtures lined the ceilings and the walls and floor seemed to be the same material as the room he was in. He listened carefully, nothing. The faint dripping had vanished, unnoticed, but the slight buzz of the lights was still there. Easing the door more open, he looked the other way. No difference, in both directions, the hallway extended twenty to thirty feet, with a few doors on either side before a t intersection. No visible signs or markers on or by the doors.

“Great,” he sighed, “no exit signs in sight. At least there’s no trouble in sight either. Now, what to do.”

He thought hard about closing the door and waiting to see what happened, but the silence and sense of emptiness led him to think that it might be a long time before something happened. Mentally, he ran over his options. Searching with the Walkman was out since the batteries were dead. He could stay where he was or explore. He wondered how long before he got thirsty or hungry. He didn’t need a bathroom at the moment, but he was sure the time would come. If nothing else, he’d like to wash up.

Stepping back into the room, he left the door cracked, allowing him to listen for others. He looked around at the spotless floor and decided. More time sitting on the floor would get him nowhere, it was time to move. He took a deep breath, turned around and opened the closed door. Stepping out into the hallway, he pulled a silver dollar from his pocket.

“Heads to the left, tails to the right.” he quietly said to himself, flipping the coin.

And missed the catch. The coin made a quiet noise as it bounced off the floor and rolled for a moment. He stepped over to pick it up and his toe moved far enough to kick the coin a little ways down the hall. With a sigh, he listened for a moment, nothing. He went to step over to the coin and it wasn’t there. He paused, looked again. Nothing. He carefully scanned the hall, no coin.

He took a step back to rescan the hallway from where he had been. As his foot came down, he felt something under his foot move. He lifted his foot and saw the coin. Where it landed, not where it had been kicked to. Slowly, he reached down and picked up the coin. A slight shiver went through his body as he wondered.

Flipping the coin again, he made the catch and checked the results. Heads, he turned to go back past the room he had come out of. He started to walk toward the intersection ahead but stopped after a few steps. He looked back and realized that the hallway looked exactly the same as far as he could see. The hair on the back of his neck rose. He’d left the door to “his” room open. All he could see was closed doors. He’d heard no clicks, no sounds.

He thought carefully, he knew he’d left that door open. He’d wanted to be able to listen. He’d left it cracked to listen, but had to reopen it to leave the room. No click, no sound, just a closed door.

“Fuck.” he whispered, staring down the hall, “…Twice.”

Cautiously, he reached out for the sliver knob. Wrapping his hand gently around it, his palm covering the faint silver paw print centered on the knob. The knob turned easily, the door swinging silently opened. This was the first room with something notable in it. Before him, framed repeating silver bands that arched across the top, was a full-size mirror.

Rather than reflecting his image though, it acted as a window. The beige floor with silver borders and white chips in geometric patterns wasn’t reflect either. Rather it was a similarly neutral floor with tiles, banded in navy or burgundy stripes, shaped into geometric patterns. Covered with a layer of familiar grime, it was the first familiar thing he’d seen. He could people moving in and out and simple benches pushed against the more ornate walls. Between the moving people, he could see the far side of the room. On a bench was a familiar cat, staring at him.

He smiled, relaxed his shoulders and stepped forward.

Inspiration / Source

The Lonely Halls are not a place built by human hands. They are Paradox given architecture.

Endless white corridors beneath merciless light. Doors spaced with perfect precision. Rooms that contain nothing at all.

Once, in a moment of terror, someone begged the universe— “Please, don’t notice me.”

The Lonely Halls are where that prayer was answered.
